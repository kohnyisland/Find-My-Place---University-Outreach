## 1. Social Media (Instagram) {#social-media}

**Formality**: Very low | **Energy**: High | **Technical depth**: None

### Produce 2–3 caption variations for social requests.

**Dominant format — POV videos**
The most-used and highest-performing format. Structure: "POV: [relatable student scenario]" with video doing the storytelling. Caption reacts to the video, it doesn't explain it.

**Caption length**
- Organic content: 2–8 words
- Partnership/event posts: can go longer, but still punchy

**ALL CAPS usage**
Reserved for genuine moments only — announcements, big news, on-campus energy. Not decorative.
- ✅ "BIG NEWS 🔥" / "FINALLY!!!"
- ❌ "WE HAVE THE BEST LISTINGS!!!"

**Emoji use**
Functional, not decorative. End-of-sentence punctuation or standalone reactions.
- 😂 humor | 😍 warmth/aspiration | 😤 relatable frustration | 🔥 hype

**Hashtags**
Always 4–6. Constants: #findmyplace #fyp #collegelife #reel

**Comment-trigger mechanic**
Use intentionally — roughly 1 in 4 posts. Format: "Comment '[keyword]' to get [thing]"
Example: "Comment 'built4life'" for a BYU housing resource

**Pop culture / meme use**
- ✅ When the reference is precise and earns its use (e.g., Dwight/The Office for "educated but homeless")
- ❌ Trend-chasing formats that date quickly or require the audience to already be in on it
- Test: does this reference land the point better than plain language would?

**Content mix target**
- 40% relatable POV content
- 30% apartment & rent insights
- 15% educational housing tips
- 10% product/platform awareness
- 5% partnerships/events

**Self-reply for humor**: FMP sometimes replies to its own post with a punchline — adds personality, rewards comment-readers.

**Real examples that worked**:
- "Just thinking about it…" (838 likes — understatement + universal relatability)
- "FINE! I'll do it myself!! 😤" (relatable frustration, specific to roommates)
- "Not the mold migration😂" (specific, funny, honest)
- "She's my guardian angel" (warmth)
- "The best feeling 😍😍" (aspirational)

**Do's**: POV format, ALL CAPS for real moments, emoji as punctuation, very short captions, comment-trigger mechanic, self-replies for humor
**Don'ts**: Long captions on organic content, press release energy, 7+ hashtags, forced positivity, trend-chasing slang

## 2. Email — Student-Facing {#email-student}

**Formality**: Low–Medium | **Energy**: Medium–High | **Technical depth**: Low

Newsletter brand name: "Nwsflash" — signals casual internal naming culture.

### Subject Lines

**Patterns that perform**:

1. **Insider tip / pattern interrupt**
   "The spring housing hack nobody talks about" / "Most students wait until January. Big mistake."
   → Positions FMP as the person who knows what everyone else doesn't. Reader feels let in.

2. **Urgency without desperation**
   "Find My Place: UVU Winner Announced TODAY" / "Winner Announced at 4PM!"
   → Real deadline, time-stamped. No fake scarcity.

3. **Speed/feature benefit + CAPS**
   "Finding housing just got FASTER"
   → Single-word CAPS on the benefit, not the brand. Feels like a text, not a blast.

4. **Cultural tie-ins**
   "Game Day Starts With The Right Place"
   → Hooks into the student calendar. Relevance is earned, not forced.

5. **Warm appreciation**
   "Thanks for coming to the Find My Place Housing Fair!"
   → Same warmth, lower energy. Matches the moment.

**Subject line rules**:
- 6–9 words ideal, 12 max
- Lead with what the student gains
- One emphasis technique per subject line (CAPS, question, or statement — never all three)
- No exclamation marks in every subject line — save for real moments
- Never: "We're excited to share..." (brand first, not reader)

### Email Body

- Open with "Hey" — it's fine, it's on-brand
- Short paragraphs, one idea each
- Active voice throughout
- Single CTA — clear, direct, uses "Find my place" over "Get started"
- Reassurance phrases when relevant: "Don't stress." / "Don't worry, though."
- No hollow openers, no corporate pleasantries

## 3. Blog Posts {#blog}

**Formality**: Low–Medium | **Energy**: Medium | **Technical depth**: Low–Medium

**Opening pattern** (from live copy):
"Hey, finding the perfect place to live isn't easy — especially when [specific situation]."

- "Hey" opener: immediately conversational
- Acknowledge reality without dramatizing
- "Life happens" is empathetic shorthand that doesn't need elaboration
- No preamble — get to the useful thing fast

**Tone throughout**:
- The upperclassman who's been there: practical, specific, a little dry
- Reassurance phrases: "Don't stress." / "Don't worry, though." — use naturally, not formulaically
- Humor through specificity, not performance
  - ✅ "you can offer to buy them a McDonald's Hot n' Spicy" (specific, unexpected, genuinely funny)
  - ❌ "landlords can be a real handful lol"

**Note on recent blog content**:
Recent FMP blog posts (Feb–Mar 2026) are AI-generated SEO content for external universities — not authoritative voice models. Use older authentic posts (e.g., "How to Sell Your Contract," Apr 2023) as voice reference, not recent SEO content.

## 4. Student-Facing Copy (General) {#student-general}

**Formality**: Low | **Energy**: Medium–High

**Lead with the problem, not the product**:
- ✅ "Apartment shopping is a nightmare. Here's the shortcut."
- ❌ "At Find My Place, we offer a comprehensive platform..."

**CTA preferences**:
- "Find my place" > "Get started" > "Sign up now"
- "Find my place" uses the brand name as a verb + personal ("my")

**Platform headline style**:
- Lowercase, casual, specific about the value
- "find housing that actually fits your life" — "actually" signals FMP vs. inadequate alternatives

**Audience note**:
FMP serves 18–35, including single students and early married/family housing — not just traditional college apartments. Tone flexes slightly for the older end (more value-oriented, less trend-heavy) but core voice is consistent.

## 5. UX / Microcopy {#ux}

**Formality**: Low | **Energy**: Medium

**Voice markers**: Casual greetings and transitions that feel human, not system-generated.
- "Howdy" / "Glad to see you back" / "Hi roomie"

**High-stakes moments** (delete, payment, errors):
- Stay in the friendly register — this is exactly when corporate coldness does the most damage
- State consequences plainly, not in legalese

**Live example** (delete modal):
"Whoa, are you sure you want to delete your account? Deleting your account can't be undone, and all your data will disappear."
- "Whoa" signals humanity
- Consequences stated plainly
- No legalese

**Do's**: Casual greetings, light wit that earns a smile, plain language for consequences, sounds human
**Don'ts**: "An error has occurred," "Action cannot be completed," jargon, humor that undermines trust at decision points

## 6. Flyers & Event Posters {#flyers}

**Formality**: Low–Medium | **Energy**: Medium–High

**Hierarchy**:
1. Lowercase Poppins headline — the single most important thing
2. Key detail (date, location, what it is)
3. CTA or supporting info
4. FMP logo with proper whitespace

**Design rules**:
- Blue (#0980ff) as dominant brand color
- White text on blue backgrounds; dark text (#262626) on white/light backgrounds
- Flat illustration style if imagery is needed (see visual-identity.md)
- Single clear message — no visual clutter
- No more than 2 typefaces (Poppins + Raleway)

**Copy rules**:
- Headline in lowercase Poppins
- Direct and specific: "housing fair this thursday" not "Join Us for an Exciting Upcoming Event"
- Energy matches the event — a casual meetup ≠ a formal announcement

## 7. Parent-Facing Content {#parents}

**Formality**: Medium | **Energy**: Medium | **Technical depth**: Low–Medium

**The shift**: Same FMP voice, but register moves from "peer" to "trusted resource." Warmer, slightly more formal. Acknowledge the emotional weight without being patronizing.

**Opening approach**: Acknowledge the stakes → reassure with substance, not platitudes.
- ✅ "Your student's first apartment is a big deal. Here's what to look for — and what questions to ask before they sign."
- ❌ "We understand this is an exciting time for your family!"

**Do's**:
- Speak to safety, reliability, and trust
- Be specific about how FMP reduces risk
- Don't talk down to parents — they're intelligent adults making a real decision

**Don'ts**:
- Dismissive of their concerns
- Student slang or meme culture
- Overly casual about a high-stakes decision
- Exclamation points (max one, ideally zero)

## 8. B2B / Property Manager Outreach {#b2b}

**Formality**: Casual-professional | **Energy**: Medium | **Technical depth**: Medium

Same FMP DNA — candid, confident, never corporate — but register shifts to professional peer, not trusted upperclassman.

**Opening formula** (from live cold call script):
"Hey [name], it's just [name] with Find My Place. I work with [named clients]. We help the properties next to campus fill with college students. Have you heard our name tossed around at all?"
- "It's just [name]" — signals no hard sell
- Named clients in sentence one — social proof immediately
- Closing question invites soft, low-stakes response

**Core B2B value prop**: Fill units with high-quality, high-converting student leads. Outcomes-based, not features-based.

**Discovery before pitch**: Ask about their current approach before offering a solution.

**The ask is always soft**: "20 minutes to see if it would be a good fit — or not." The "or not" signals genuine qualifying intent, not pressure.

**Objection handling**:
- "Send me info" stall: "Mind if I ask a brutally honest question? When folks tell me to send them info, they're usually just too nice to tell me to go away. Is that what's happening here?"
- "Not sure it's a fit": "Here's our deal — I'll send you the info and if you want to take 20 minutes to see if it would be a good fit — or not — I'll send you a calendar invite. If you don't like it, you can just decline. Fair?"

**Data to use**: "almost up to 30% conversion rate," "high-quality, high-converting leads"

**Do's**: Open with "Hey," name existing clients early (EMG, AMC, Redstone), ask discovery questions first, soft close, handle objections with warmth and light humor
**Don'ts**: Student slang or meme culture, hard-close pressure tactics, lead with features instead of outcomes, overpromise, robotic rebuttals
